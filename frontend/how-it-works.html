From RFQ to Quote

The current quoting process

The quoting process at TMI is assumed to be entirely manual. It relies on experienced engineers to translate incoming project requirements into a detailed and accurate price. Below is a step-by-step walkthrough of how that process works today and where the time is lost.

1
Requirements arrive
A data center developer sends TMI their project specifications — the scale of the facility, cooling requirements, configuration preferences, location, and timeline. It arrives as an unstructured document written by a human for a human.
2
Engineer reviews the requirements
An experienced engineer reads the document and begins translating it into something workable. They reference standard designs stored as CAD files and start customising those designs for the specific project. Decisions about which configurations to use, which components fit, and how to handle constraints all happen here — entirely in the engineer's head.
3
Cost logic is developed
Once the engineer understands what needs to be built, they work out what it costs — materials, labour at each fabrication stage, and any project-specific factors. This reasoning is not documented anywhere. It is reconstructed from experience every single time.
4
Logic is entered into the CPQ system
The engineering and cost logic is then translated into TMI's homegrown CPQ system. Because it is purpose-built for their specific workflow, this step requires either an in-house developer or someone who understands the system deeply enough to encode the logic correctly — an additional translation layer between what the engineer knows and what the system can execute.
5
CPQ produces the quote
Once the logic is in the system, the CPQ generates the final price quote. This part of the process works well. The CPQ is built specifically for TMI's level of customisation and is capable of producing detailed, granular output. The bottleneck is everything that happens before the CPQ can do its job.
Where the time goes

The time is lost in Steps 2 and 3. Every new project requires an engineer to reconstruct the full reasoning process from scratch. There is no system that remembers how similar projects were handled, no way to reuse logic across similar requests, and no mechanism to capture what an experienced engineer knows so that it can be applied consistently.

Two-call generation pipeline

The system addresses Steps 2 and 3 directly. When a new RFQ comes in, it retrieves the most relevant historical quotes and engineering rules, runs them through two sequential Claude calls to produce an engineering brief and a full quote draft, then surfaces the output for engineer review before anything reaches the CPQ.

Step 1
RFQ input & retrieval
The engineer fills in 6 project fields. The retrieval agent queries ChromaDB to pull the 3 most similar historical quotes and the 3 most relevant engineering rules.
Step 2
Call 1 — engineering brief
A blocking Claude Sonnet call receives the RFQ, retrieved history, and rules. It returns a structured engineering brief inside <engineering_brief> tags, streamed to the UI as an SSE event.
Step 3
Call 2 — quote draft
A second Claude call takes only the engineering brief and streams the full 7-section quote directly to the browser. Tag stripping removes wrapper markup before it reaches the UI.
Step 4
Parallel evaluation
A mechanical evaluator runs 4 programmatic checks (pricing formula, section presence, word count). An LLM judge scores 5 quality dimensions and returns a pass/flag/fail verdict.
Step 5
Human review
The engineer edits, approves, or regenerates individual sections. SME routing lets them assign sections to specialists. All changes are tracked.
Step 6
CPQ export & correction loop
Approved quotes export as a bill of materials into the CPQ system. Engineer corrections feed back into the prompt assembly as a nightly improvement loop (planned).

Data sources

The AI does not generate from general knowledge. Everything it produces is grounded in two TMI-specific databases built before the system was deployed.

Quote library
30
Historical TMI quote PDFs, each extracted into plain text and stored as a vector embedding. When a new RFQ arrives, the three most similar historical quotes are retrieved and included in the prompt.
Rule library
356
Engineering rules extracted from TMI's internal standards. Each rule is stored as a separate embedding. The three rules most relevant to the incoming RFQ are retrieved and passed to Call 1.
Step 1
30 synthetic quote PDFs
Generated using real industry research into how data center cooling RFQs are structured and what drives cost in custom air handling systems. Not real TMI data, but grounded in real industry patterns.

Step 2
Claude extracts and deduplicates rules
Claude reads the engineering notes and critical design decisions from all 30 quotes, pulls out reusable standalone rules from each document, then runs a deduplication pass across all raw outputs to remove near-duplicates.
→
Step 3
356 rules embedded into ChromaDB
The result is a rule library that captures distilled engineering judgment from the entire quote corpus — not just individual documents. Retrievable by semantic similarity at generation time.

When real TMI quote data becomes available it gets added to the corpus, extract_rules.py runs again, and the knowledge base updates automatically.

How retrieval works: Both libraries use sentence-transformer embeddings stored in ChromaDB. When an RFQ is submitted, its text is embedded using the same model and compared against every stored document using cosine similarity. The top 3 matches from each library are returned with their similarity scores. Nothing is retrieved randomly — every document in the prompt earned its place against the specific RFQ.

How the output is evaluated

The generated quote is not passed directly to the engineer. Two parallel evaluators run first — one programmatic, one AI — so that obvious errors are flagged before human review begins.

Mechanical checks
Pricing arithmetic — verifies that Overhead = (Materials + Labour) × 18% and Total = Subtotal ÷ 0.78, with figures pulled directly from the quote.
Section presence — confirms all 7 required section headings appear in the correct order.
Word count — flags quotes that are significantly under or over expected length, which typically signals a truncated or padded response.
Bill of materials completeness — checks that Section 3 contains line items with quantities and unit costs, not vague placeholders.
LLM judge — 5 dimensions (scored 1–5)
Section completeness — are all required content areas covered, even if headings differ?
Technical specificity — are coil configuration, fan type, controls, and filtration appropriate for the stated cooling load and redundancy tier?
Location awareness — does the engineering notes section include climate conditions, local codes, and regional factors specific to the project location?
Pricing coherence — do the line items and figures reflect the actual equipment scope and match the reference pricing from similar historical quotes?
Style match — does the draft read like a professional TMI quotation, with precise language and properly flagged assumptions?

The correction loop

Every engineer edit logs the original AI output and the corrected version to corrections.json. This data is already structured for future use — running rule extraction against the corrections periodically would fold human judgment back into the knowledge base and raise the baseline for every future quote. Currently captured, not yet automated.

What Claude is being asked to do

Each call gives Claude a distinct role and a constrained set of instructions. The key design choice is separation: Call 1 does only engineering judgment, Call 2 does only formatting and arithmetic. Neither call tries to do both.

Call 1 Engineering brief — blocking, not streamed
System role
"You are a senior applications engineer at TMI Climate Solutions. Your job at this stage is engineering judgment only — not documentation, not pricing. You think before you write."
Key instructions
1
Identify the two historical precedents most similar in cooling load and configuration type — state why each is relevant and what is materially different about this RFQ.
2
Define the full equipment configuration: cooling load derivation, unit type, coil spec, fan arrangement, filtration grade, controls, and redundancy tier implications.
3
Surface every assumption where the RFQ is underspecified — what is unknown, what is assumed, and why that assumption is reasonable.
4
Flag location-specific requirements: applicable codes, climate design conditions, and any regional environmental or water quality factors.
Call 2 Quote draft — streamed in 7 sections
System role
"Your engineering decisions have already been made and are captured in the brief below. Your job now is to express those decisions precisely and completely in TMI's standard quotation format. Formatting accuracy and pricing arithmetic are the two things that cannot have errors."
Key instructions
1
Write all 7 sections in order: Project Scope Summary, Equipment Specification, Bill of Materials, Pricing Summary, Lead Time and Schedule, Commercial Terms, Engineering Notes.
2
Section 3 (Bill of Materials) must list every line item with quantity, unit cost, and extended cost — no vague placeholders.
3
Section 4 (Pricing Summary) must apply the formula explicitly: Materials + Labour + Overhead = Subtotal, Subtotal ÷ 0.78 = Total, shown as separate line items.
4
Before closing, verify pricing arithmetic, section heading order, and assumption coverage inside <verification> tags. Do not close the response until verification is complete.

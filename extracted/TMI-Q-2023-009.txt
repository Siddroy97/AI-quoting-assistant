1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 340 kW sensible at Nashville design conditions — outdoor DB 36°C / WB 24°C, entering air 23°C DB / 50% RH.
Three (3) units at 170 kW each, N+1: two active, one standby on 168-hour auto-rotation.
Configuration: Three (3) roof-mounted split DX precision cooling units with integrated tenant energy sub-metering. In a carrier-neutral
colocation environment, the facility operator bills tenants for cooling proportionally; metered cooling energy output is a contractual
billing instrument. Each unit carries an integrated Class 0.5S revenue-grade kWh meter with Modbus TCP output reporting to the
facility's DCIM platform.
Refrigerant circuit: R-410A, dual-circuit scroll compressors per unit, 6-row aluminium-fin copper-tube evaporator coil. At Nashville's
36°C DB / 24°C WB design, running both circuits simultaneously at reduced speed produces a lower evaporator coil surface
temperature across the full fin face area, improving moisture removal versus single-circuit cycling.
Condensing: Remote air-cooled condenser, roof-mounted, EC fans with variable-speed head-pressure control, per unit. Nashville
design conditions are moderate; condenser selection includes standard 10% capacity margin.
Tenant sub-metering: integrated Class 0.5S revenue-grade kWh meter per unit per IEC 62053-22. Reports instantaneous power,
cumulative kWh, and power factor via Modbus TCP at 1-minute intervals. Modbus register map pre-configured to the DCIM platform
specification supplied by the customer at quotation.
Controls: N+1 group controller, BACnet IP, 168-hour rotation, with Modbus TCP integration for sub-metering data aggregation across
all three units to the DCIM gateway.
Airflow: 16,000 CFM per unit at 0.60 in. w.g. Overhead supply plenum, raised-floor return. EC fan motor, variable speed via
supply-air temperature control.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 75 mm double-wall insulated.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-11, differential pressure monitoring.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Nashville ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 36°C DB / 24°C WB. The 24°C WB creates a seasonal latent load limited primarily to June
through September. The dual-circuit coil handles this load without requiring the deep-dehumidification coil geometry demanded by
tropical climates.
(cid:127) Revenue-grade sub-metering at Class 0.5S accuracy: in a carrier-neutral colocation environment, cooling energy metering is a
billing instrument. Class 0.5S means accuracy within +/- 0.5% of reading across the operating load range. TMI sources sub-meters
from manufacturers with a minimum 10-year field reliability record; substituting a lower-cost meter risks billing disputes and
unexpected replacement costs within the equipment service life.
(cid:127) Dual-circuit simultaneous operation: running both circuits at 60% speed simultaneously rather than cycling one at 100% and one off
produces a lower evaporator coil surface temperature across the full fin face area, deepening dew-point depression and removing
more moisture per unit of cooling energy — a subtle but measurable advantage when humidity management matters seasonally.
(cid:127) BAFO analysis: TMI's BAFO reduction of 4.1% represented the full scope achievable through procurement optimisation at current
production volume. Further reduction would have required either a margin concession below TMI's floor or component substitution
that TMI's quality standards did not permit.
(cid:127) EC motor qualification: TMI qualifies EC fan motors on a 10-year mean-time-between-failure dataset from North American
deployments. Motors without this dataset are not accepted into the TMI supply chain regardless of price, as EC motor failures in data
centre cooling applications generate disproportionate service costs relative to the original component cost savings.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was integrating Class 0.5S revenue-grade sub-metering at the
factory rather than treating it as a field-installed accessory — ensuring the Modbus register map, calibration certificate, and DCIM
integration are verified before the equipment ships and eliminating the commissioning-stage integration failures that commonly arise
when metering hardware is sourced and installed independently of the cooling unit manufacturer.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost on price after a best-and-final-offer process. TMI submitted a BAFO 4.1% below the initial quotation, representing the full
savings achievable without margin compression. The competitor's BAFO came in 6.8% below TMI's revised price. Post-award
feedback indicated the competitor had sourced EC condenser fan assemblies from a lower-cost supplier — a substitution TMI was
unwilling to make given the 10-year field reliability threshold TMI requires for EC motor qualification.

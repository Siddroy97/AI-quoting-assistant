1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 1,400 kW sensible with 420 kW latent removal — Miami wet-season design conditions, outdoor DB 34°C / WB
27°C, entering air 26°C DB / 65% RH. Four (4) large AHUs at 450 kW sensible each, N+1: three active, one standby on 168-hour
rotation. Miami's 27°C WB is among the highest in the continental US and creates a dominant latent load that governs the coil
selection more than the sensible load alone.
Cooling coil — enhanced deep dehumidification: 10-row, 14 fins per inch, aluminium-fin copper-tube; face area 2,200 mm x 1,500
mm; CHW supply 6°C / return 12°C. This configuration achieves a leaving air condition of 12°C DB / 10°C DP. The 10°C DP is 2°C
below the space dew-point target of 12°C DP, providing a buffer for CHW supply temperature variation. A standard 8-row coil at 7°C
supply would leave air at 13°C DP — insufficient to offset Miami's 65% RH entering air.
Corrosion protection — epoxy-coated coil fins: aluminium fins are coated with a 25-micron epoxy polymer before assembly into the
coil matrix. This coating prevents the salt-accelerated oxidation reaction that causes pitting corrosion on uncoated fins in coastal
environments. TMI's Miami reference installations with epoxy fins show no measurable fin erosion at 7-year inspection; uncoated
aluminium fins in the same environment showed 15 to 20% fin loss within 4 years.
Corrosion protection — e-coat cabinet interior: all interior cabinet steel surfaces are treated with an electro-deposited polyurethane
coating before assembly. E-coat provides a uniform 20-micron film on recessed areas, weld seams, and formed edges where
spray-applied coatings do not penetrate. E-coat interior plus powder-coat exterior is the minimum corrosion protection standard TMI
specifies for coastal installations.
Condensate management: stainless steel drain pan per unit with capacity of 1.2 litres per minute — sized for maximum condensate
generation at 65% RH entering air and 10°C leaving dew-point. Float switch and overflow alarm on each pan.
Fan section: three (3) backward-curved EC fans per unit, 58,000 CFM, variable-speed drives. Tropical-rated Class H insulation motor
windings — standard Class F windings are susceptible to moisture penetration in sustained high-RH environments.
Controls: N+1 group controller, BACnet IP, dew-point control with 0.5°C resolution sensors. Dew-point high alarm at 13°C DP; group
controller calls standby unit if any active unit cannot maintain supply dew-point within 0.5°C of setpoint for more than 10 minutes.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-13 with moisture-resistant polyester media. Standard cellulose MERV-13 media collapses when saturated
with condensation during high-humidity start-up events; polyester media retains structural integrity when wet.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Miami ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 34°C DB / 27°C WB. The 27°C WB is the design driver. At 27°C WB, outdoor air moisture
content is approximately 18.5 g/kg — one of the highest in any major US market. Inside the data hall, equipment heat generation
keeps relative humidity below 50% at typical air change rates, but any infiltration pathway from the exterior introduces this
high-moisture air directly into the IT space.
(cid:127) Dew-point control at 10°C DP: the coil surface temperature at 6°C CHW supply is approximately 8°C. Moist entering air contacts the
fin surface at 8°C — well below its dew-point of approximately 16°C at 65% RH — and moisture condenses aggressively on the first
two rows of the 10-row coil. The remaining 8 rows provide sensible cooling of the now-drier air. This condensation-forward design is
correct for a high-latent tropical load; it would not be appropriate for a dry Southwest climate.
(cid:127) Epoxy fin coating durability: TMI's field data from Miami and Fort Lauderdale reference installations shows that epoxy-coated fins at
year 7 post-installation have a fin metal loss of less than 2% versus 15 to 20% for uncoated aluminium in the same buildings. The
differential is driven by salt aerosol concentration in Miami's marine air mass, which is approximately 3 to 4 times higher than a
non-coastal urban environment.
(cid:127) MERV-13 polyester media: standard cellulose MERV-13 filter media becomes structurally compromised when relative humidity at
the filter face exceeds 90% during building start-up after a weekend shutdown. Polyester media retains its structural geometry when
wet, maintaining design pressure drop and particle capture efficiency. The cost premium over cellulose MERV-13 is approximately
CAD 45 per filter change.
(cid:127) CHW supply at 6°C is critical for achieving the 10°C leaving dew-point. If CHW supply rises above 7°C, the dew-point control target
cannot be maintained and condensation risk on IT equipment increases. The customer's chiller plant setpoint must hold 6°C under
peak load; this constraint was communicated to the customer's mechanical engineer.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was pairing the 10-row, 14 FPI deep dehumidification coil at
6°C/12°C CHW delta-T with epoxy fin coating and e-coat cabinet interior — treating the Miami corrosion environment as a primary
design constraint equal in importance to the thermal design — because the thermal performance of a TMI standard unit in Miami
would degrade below specified capacity within 4 years as uncoated aluminium fins corrode and their effective heat transfer area is
reduced.
OUTCOME: WON
Awarded on technical merit. TMI's tropical-climate engineering package — specifically the epoxy-coated coil fins, e-coat cabinet
interior, and deep dehumidification coil configuration — directly addressed the customer's documented history of corrosion-related
coil failures and humidity excursions at their existing Miami facility. Competing bids offered standard construction with no
tropical-specific provisions. The customer's facilities director indicated that the cost of two previous coil replacements at the existing
site exceeded the price premium of TMI's tropical package.

1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 2,800 kW sensible — Phase 1 of a three-phase campus totalling 12,000 kW. Eight (8) large chilled water AHUs,
450 kW sensible each, Tier 3 2N configuration: four active units, four standby on 120-hour auto-rotation.
Configuration: Large floor-mounted chilled water AHUs with hot-aisle containment. CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C. Zone group
controller is designed for expansion to a 24-unit total system to accommodate Phase 2 and Phase 3 additions — no controller
replacement required as the campus builds out.
Cooling coil: 10-row, 12 fins per inch, aluminium-fin copper-tube; face area 2,100 mm x 1,400 mm; CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C.
The 10-row depth is required at this delta-T to achieve a leaving dew-point of 11°C for Dallas's humidity profile at 24°C WB design
condition.
Fan section: three (3) EC backward-curved fans per unit, 48,000 CFM total at 0.85 in. w.g. Three-fan design for fault tolerance: single
fan failure reduces airflow by 33%, managed by the group controller increasing adjacent unit fan speed without triggering an
automatic N failover.
Waterside economiser: modulating bypass valve on CHW supply line, activated when an outdoor WB sensor array reads below
13°C. Partial free-cooling mode allows mixed operation at outdoor WB between 10°C and 13°C — approximately 1,200 hours per
year in Dallas.
Controls: zone group controller, BACnet IP, DCIM API, 120-hour 2N rotation. Expandable controller architecture: current 8-unit
deployment occupies 8 of 24 available unit addresses. Phase 2 and 3 units connect to the same controller infrastructure.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 100 mm double-wall insulated.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-13.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Dallas ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 40°C DB / 24°C WB. The 24°C WB drives the 10-row coil depth requirement; a standard
8-row coil at this humidity would not achieve the 11°C leaving dew-point without reducing CHW supply below 6°C.
(cid:127) Waterside economiser at 1,200 annual hours: Dallas's warm climate provides significantly fewer free-cooling hours than Pacific
Northwest or Canadian sites. The economiser provides meaningful compressor energy savings during November to March but is not
the primary efficiency driver for a Dallas installation — it is specified primarily for Tier 3 design resilience and partial-load cost
reduction.
(cid:127) Expandable zone controller architecture: the current 8-unit Phase 1 deployment uses 8 of 24 available unit addresses. Phase 2 and
Phase 3 units connect directly to the same controller infrastructure without hardware replacement. This commitment was made at the
Phase 1 engineering stage at no additional cost and eliminates a controller replacement estimated at CAD 38,000 per zone at full
campus buildout.
(cid:127) Lead time analysis: TMI's 20-week standard lead time is dominated by the 7-week custom coil fabrication lead from the coil
supplier. Pre-purchasing the coils before drawing approval would have reduced total lead time to 14 weeks but exposed TMI to a coil
replacement cost of approximately CAD 120,000 if a post-approval drawing change modified the coil specification. For a project
yielding approximately CAD 175,000 in gross margin, this risk was not commercially acceptable.
(cid:127) Competitor proximity advantage: a Dallas-area fabrication facility reduces the competitor's coil procurement lead time from 7 weeks
(Montreal-to-Dallas import) to approximately 3 weeks (local fabrication). This structural supply chain advantage is a sustainable
competitive factor for hyperscale campus projects in the Texas market where go-live schedule drives contract awards.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was architecting the zone group controller for 24-unit
expandability from the Phase 1 outset, so that Phase 2 and Phase 3 AHU groups can be added to the same controller without
hardware replacement — a forward-compatibility investment that adds no cost to Phase 1 but eliminates a controller replacement
cost estimated at CAD 38,000 per zone when the campus reaches full 12,000 kW buildout.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost on lead time. The competing manufacturer had a fabrication facility in the Dallas area and could guarantee delivery in 14 weeks.
TMI's standard lead time for this configuration was 20 weeks and the expedite path reduced this to 17 weeks at best. In a hyperscale
programme where server deployment follows cooling system commissioning by 3 weeks, the 6-week lead time difference
represented a direct delay in revenue recognition on the customer's server lease agreements — a financial impact the customer's
CFO quantified at approximately USD 1.8 million in foregone revenue, which exceeded the cost differential between the two bids.

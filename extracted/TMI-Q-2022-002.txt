1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 320 kW sensible with 85 kW latent removal at Charlotte design conditions — outdoor DB 37°C / WB 24°C, entering
air 23°C DB / 45% RH, chilled water supply 7°C / return 14°C.
Configuration: Two (2) floor-mounted chilled water computer room air handling units (CRAHs), 160 kW sensible each, arranged in N
redundancy with top-discharge plenum into a common ceiling return. The Charlotte climate presents a sensible heat ratio challenge:
summer wet-bulb peaks at 24°C, which drives a meaningful latent load inside the data hall from infiltration and personnel. A chilled
water coil selected at 7°C supply and 14°C return achieves a leaving dew-point of 11°C, delivering adequate dehumidification that a
packaged DX system at equivalent sensible capacity cannot reliably sustain.
Cooling coil: 6-row, 12 fins per inch, aluminium-fin copper-tube chilled water coil; face area 1,400 mm x 900 mm; coil selected to
achieve leaving air conditions of 13°C DB / 11°C DP at design load, providing a sensible heat ratio of 0.79 — appropriate for
Charlotte's latent load profile.
Fan section: Backward-curved plenum fan, direct-drive EC motor, variable-speed drive integrated into motor frame. Fan is sized for
15,000 CFM at 0.60 in. w.g. external static into the top-discharge plenum; EC motor allows speed reduction to 45% minimum during
low-load overnight periods, reducing fan energy by approximately 77% at part-load.
Chilled water valve: 2-way modulating valve, 2-inch, with spring-return fail-closed actuator and 4–20 mA control signal. Valve is sized
for a 10% authority ratio at design flow to prevent hunting in the cooling control loop.
Humidity monitoring: Supply air dew-point sensor, ±0.3°C accuracy, hardwired to unit controller. High dew-point alarm triggers at
14°C DP and is forwarded to the building management system via Modbus RTU.
Controls: Stand-alone unit controller, Modbus RTU; supply air temperature setpoint 16°C; dew-point monitoring with BMS alarm
relay; sequential fan speed ramp-up on start to limit inrush.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 75 mm double-wall insulated panels, brushed stainless steel base frame and drain pan.
Top-discharge plenum adapter, galvanised, included.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-11 panel filters. MERV-11 selected over MERV-13 because the data hall has no outdoor air intake; MERV-11
provides adequate particle control with 18% lower pressure drop, reducing fan energy.
Redundancy tier: N — two units installed, one active per room, one standby. Manual changeover at unit controller.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Charlotte ASHRAE 2021 0.4% cooling design: 37°C DB / 24°C WB. The 24°C wet-bulb is the governing design parameter for this
project, not the dry-bulb. At 24°C WB the moisture content of infiltration air entering the data hall is approximately 15.6 g/kg, which is
meaningfully higher than the 10.5 g/kg target inside the space. A chilled water coil can sustain a leaving dew-point of 11°C by
deep-cooling the air across the fin surface; a packaged DX system operating at the same sensible capacity typically delivers a
leaving dew-point of 13 to 15°C, which is insufficient to offset Charlotte's summer latent load.
(cid:127) The 7°C CHW supply temperature is the minimum that drives the coil surface below the 11°C DP target. If the chiller plant delivers
warmer than 8°C supply, the leaving dew-point rises to approximately 13°C and dehumidification performance degrades. The CHW
plant design must hold 7°C supply under peak load; this constraint was communicated to the customer's mechanical engineer and is
a risk on projects where the chiller is sized tightly.
(cid:127) Valve authority: the 2-way modulating valve is sized at 10% authority, meaning the pressure drop across the valve at design flow
equals 10% of the total circuit pressure drop. Authority below 10% leads to a flat control curve where large valve stem movements
produce small flow changes, causing hunting. The spring-return fail-closed actuator ensures the coil drains and the cooling load is
shed safely on a controls power failure.
(cid:127) EC motor VSD allows fan speed to reduce to 45% of design during low-load periods (typically 23:00 to 06:00 in a commercial data
hall). Fan power scales as the cube of speed, so operation at 45% speed consumes approximately 9% of full-load fan power — a
meaningful annual energy saving in a climate where Charlotte ambient temperature is below 20°C for over 4,000 hours per year.
(cid:127) MERV-11 versus MERV-13: this data hall has no outdoor air intake and operates in a positive-pressure environment relative to
adjacent corridors. MERV-11 is appropriate for recirculated indoor air. MERV-13 would be required only if outdoor air is introduced
through an economiser section; no economiser is included in this scope.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was selecting a chilled water coil configuration at 7°C supply
with a 6-row depth and 12 FPI fin spacing to achieve a leaving dew-point of 11°C, rather than specifying a packaged DX system — a
choice that adds approximately 28% to capital cost but is the only configuration capable of controlling latent load reliably in
Charlotte's 24°C wet-bulb summer climate.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost on capital cost. The customer awarded to a competitor offering packaged direct-expansion precision cooling units at a price
approximately 23% lower. The customer acknowledged the CHW configuration's superior dehumidification control and lower long-run
operating cost but indicated that the capital budget cap was a binding constraint for this phase. The customer accepted the
operational dehumidification trade-off, noting they would revisit a CHW strategy in Phase 2 when the on-site chiller plant is confirmed.

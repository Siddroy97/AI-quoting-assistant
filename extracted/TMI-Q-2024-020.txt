1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 1,300 kW sensible — hybrid configuration; 650 kW direct-expansion primary, 650 kW chilled water supplementary;
entering air 23°C DB / 50% RH; CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C. Four (4) hybrid DX/CHW units at 325 kW each, N+1: three active,
one standby.
Configuration: Four floor-mounted hybrid DX/CHW precision cooling units replacing the existing perimeter CRACs. Unit footprint
matches existing CRAC footprint within 150 mm on all sides, minimising raised-floor cutout rework — confirmed against the as-built
drawing provided by the customer at the time of quotation.
DX primary circuit: R-410A, 6-row evaporator coil, dual-circuit scroll compressors, remote air-cooled condenser on roof. DX provides
up to 163 kW per unit at Richmond's 36°C DB design condition. DX is the base-load circuit operating continuously during occupied
hours.
CHW supplementary circuit: 4-row coil, 2-way modulating valve, CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C. Hybrid controller activates CHW
circuit when DX load exceeds 70% of DX rated capacity, or when DX compressor discharge pressure exceeds 3,800 kPa, whichever
occurs first. The 70% threshold ensures the DX compressor operates at steady mid-load rather than cycling, extending compressor
service life.
Hybrid energy strategy: operating DX as the base circuit and CHW as the supplement is consistent with Virginia's electricity tariff
structure, where demand charges are based on 15-minute peak demand. Running DX continuously at a steady-state load avoids the
demand charge spike that would occur if all CHW circuits simultaneously activated at maximum valve opening following a sudden
cooling load increase.
Controls: hybrid controller per unit, BACnet IP, with Modbus TCP for integration with the customer's existing DCIM platform. N+1
group controller, 168-hour rotation. Independent energy sub-metering on DX and CHW circuits — allowing the customer to analyse
the actual load split in operation.
Airflow: 36,000 CFM per unit at 0.75 in. w.g. Backward-curved EC fan, variable speed via supply-air temperature control.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 100 mm double-wall insulated. CRAC-compatible footprint.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-13.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Richmond ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 36°C DB / 25°C WB. Moderate summer climate; DX circuit is not stressed at design
conditions. The governing technical constraint is footprint compatibility with the existing raised-floor cutouts, which determines the
cabinet dimensions and has been verified against the as-built drawing provided by the customer.
(cid:127) Hybrid load split at 70% DX threshold: operating the DX circuit as the continuous base load and engaging CHW only above 70% DX
capacity ensures that compressor oil circulation is maintained at all times. A DX system that cycles on and off rapidly in response to
small load variations accumulates refrigerant migration stress faster than a system operating at a steady mid-load condition.
(cid:127) Independent DX and CHW energy sub-metering: the separate metering of DX and CHW circuit energy allows the customer to
analyse whether the hybrid load split is operating as designed. If the CHW circuit is engaging more frequently than the 70% threshold
predicts, it may indicate the CHW plant is delivering cooler supply temperature than the 7°C design, allowing the CHW valve to carry
more load than intended.
(cid:127) CRAC footprint matching: the existing CRAC units have an 1,100 mm x 900 mm base footprint. TMI's hybrid unit is designed at
1,150 mm x 950 mm — 50 mm wider and deeper on each axis. This marginal oversize fits within the standard inter-row spacing of
the existing layout without requiring raised-floor cutout modification.
(cid:127) Modbus TCP DCIM integration: TMI's hybrid controller provides 48 Modbus TCP registers covering supply and return air
temperatures, DX and CHW circuit status, compressor discharge pressure, fan speed, valve position, and energy meter readings for
both circuits. The register map is compatible with all major DCIM platforms. TMI does not offer DCIM analytics dashboards as a
bundled service.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was setting the DX-to-CHW handover threshold at 70% of DX
rated capacity rather than a fixed supply-air temperature threshold, ensuring the DX compressor operates at steady mid-load rather
than cycling between 100% and off — reducing compressor start-stop stress cycles and extending service life in a climate where the
DX circuit carries base load for more than 6,000 hours annually.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost because the competing bid included a bundled 2-year DCIM software licence with analytics dashboards tailored to the
customer's existing monitoring infrastructure. The customer's CIO placed significant value on the analytics capability, viewing the
combined equipment-plus-DCIM package as a strategic platform investment rather than a cooling equipment procurement. TMI's
equipment-only proposal, while technically superior in cooling capacity and energy efficiency, did not address the DCIM analytics
dimension. Post-award, the facilities director indicated that TMI's equipment was preferred on technical merit but was overruled at the
CIO level.

1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 200 kW sensible at Seattle design conditions — outdoor DB 31°C / WB 22°C, entering air 23°C DB / 50% RH.
Seattle's mild summer climate means this equipment will operate at or below 60% of rated capacity for more than 70% of annual
hours; part-load efficiency is therefore as important as peak capacity in the total cost of ownership calculation.
Configuration: Three (3) in-row direct-expansion precision cooling units, 100 kW sensible each, N+1 active rotation. In-row form
factor: each unit occupies one standard 600 mm rack-row position between server cabinet rows. Rear of unit discharges directly into
the hot aisle; front of unit draws return air from the cold aisle. Remote condenser sections are wall-mounted on the building exterior,
one per indoor unit.
Refrigerant circuit: R-410A, scroll compressor per unit, 6-row aluminium-fin copper-tube evaporator coil. Single refrigerant circuit per
unit; N+1 configuration provides the redundancy path. At Seattle's mild outdoor conditions, the compressor operates at EER values
approximately 30% above nameplate for more than half the year.
Airflow: 9,200 CFM per unit at 0.35 in. w.g. internal resistance. In-row fans are backward-curved centrifugal, direct-drive EC motor. At
Seattle's 31°C design condition the compressor unloads to approximately 75% capacity; fan speed reduces proportionally via
supply-air temperature control, cutting fan energy by nearly 42% relative to full load.
Condensing: Wall-mounted remote condenser, EC fans, one per indoor unit. Condenser is sized for 31°C outdoor DB with a 5°C
approach to target condensing temperature. Seattle's low wet-bulb means condensing pressure is rarely at the nameplate peak, and
EC condenser fan speed modulates down to 40% minimum during the many hours when outdoor DB is below 20°C.
Controls: N+1 group controller, BACnet MS/TP, 168-hour rotation. Hot-aisle temperature sensor array, four sensors per unit, provides
spatial hot-spot detection independent of supply-air temperature control. Hot-spot alarm forwards to BMS via dry-contact relay.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, in-row profile, 50 mm double-wall insulated. Unit footprint: 600 mm W x 1,050 mm D x 2,000 mm
H — standard rack-row pitch compatible.
Filtration: 2-inch MERV-11, front-access slide-out frame for filter replacement without moving the unit.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Seattle ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 31°C DB / 22°C WB. This is a mild summer climate by data centre cooling standards. The
31°C DB peak occurs fewer than 35 hours per year; outdoor DB is below 25°C for approximately 5,200 hours annually. A unit
selected for Phoenix or Atlanta would be operating at chronically low part-load factors in Seattle, degrading compressor oil circulation
and increasing refrigerant migration risk.
(cid:127) Part-load performance dominates the life-cycle cost at this site. At 60% cooling load and 22°C outdoor DB, the scroll compressor
operates at an EER approximately 35% above its 31°C design nameplate — an advantage that accumulates significantly over a
15-year equipment life but is invisible in a capital-cost-only evaluation.
(cid:127) In-row form factor is appropriate for this rack-row geometry because the hot-aisle width is 900 mm — below the minimum
recommended for floor-mounted precision units with front access panels. The in-row unit occupies one rack position and discharges
laterally into the hot aisle, eliminating the floor-space conflict.
(cid:127) Pre-charged refrigerant line sets are specified for a maximum 15 m run from indoor unit to wall condenser. If the wall mounting
location requires a run longer than 15 m, field-brazed piping and refrigerant top-up will be required.
(cid:127) Hot-aisle sensor array provides spatial temperature resolution across four rack-bay positions. A single supply-air temperature
sensor would not detect a localised hot spot caused by a high-density blade server chassis in one bay while adjacent bays remain
within setpoint.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was optimising the EC fan and compressor selection for
Seattle's 31°C design condition rather than applying a standard North American mid-continent selection — a configuration that
delivers 30% higher EER at typical Seattle part-load conditions and reduces annual cooling electricity consumption by an estimated
18% relative to a generic selection, though this life-cycle advantage was not reflected in the customer's capital-cost-only evaluation.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost on price. A competing manufacturer submitted a bid 8.6% below TMI's total. Post-award feedback indicated price was the sole
evaluation criterion; technical differentiation was not weighted. The customer acknowledged TMI's Pacific Northwest
climate-optimised EC fan specification and higher part-load EER were technically superior but confirmed the budget cap was binding.

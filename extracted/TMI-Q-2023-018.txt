1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 850 kW sensible — CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C, entering air 23°C DB / 50% RH. Three (3) large chilled water
AHUs at 425 kW sensible each, N+1: two active, one standby on 168-hour auto-rotation.
Configuration: Floor-mounted chilled water AHUs, raised-floor supply, overhead return. Portland's climate is exceptional for data
centre cooling economics: outdoor wet-bulb is below 10°C for approximately 5,600 hours per year — nearly two-thirds of annual
hours — enabling sustained waterside free-cooling through the CHW plant's fluid cooler bypass circuit. TMI's unit controls include a
pre-wired waterside economiser interface enabling full or partial free-cooling operation without unit-level modification.
Cooling coil: 8-row, 12 fins per inch, aluminium-fin copper-tube; face area 2,000 mm x 1,300 mm; CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C. Coil
face velocity is 2.8 m/s, below the standard 3.0 m/s maximum, reducing coil pressure drop and fan energy — justified by Portland's
high proportion of part-load operating hours.
Fan section: two (2) backward-curved EC fans per unit, 44,000 CFM at 0.80 in. w.g. EC motor selection is optimised for Portland's
part-load profile; the motor is selected at its highest efficiency point at 60% speed, corresponding to the dominant operating condition
(low ambient, free-cooling active, reduced supply air temperature demand).
Waterside economiser interface: pre-wired BACnet binary input for the CHW plant economiser enable signal. When enabled, unit
controller reduces fan speed to maintain a relaxed supply air setpoint of 18°C versus 16°C in mechanical cooling mode; the higher
setpoint reduces fan speed to approximately 50%, cutting fan energy by approximately 87% relative to full-load mechanical cooling.
Controls: N+1 group controller, BACnet IP, 168-hour rotation; supply temperature and dew-point; energy metering per unit. Waterside
economiser logic in group controller: when economiser enable signal is active, supply temperature setpoint is automatically raised to
18°C and fan speed ramps down smoothly.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 100 mm double-wall insulated.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-13. Portland air quality is affected by West Coast wildfire smoke during August and September; MERV-13
captures fine particulate matter (PM2.5) at greater than 85% efficiency and is TMI's standard specification for all Pacific Northwest
data centre applications.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Portland ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 35°C DB / 23°C WB. The moderate summer peak means the system is thermally
understressed; the 850 kW cooling load would be delivered at well below the coil's peak thermal duty for the vast majority of
operating hours.
(cid:127) Waterside economiser at 5,600 annual hours: Portland's climate makes this one of the highest free-cooling-potential sites in North
America. For comparison, the same economiser interface in Dallas provides approximately 1,200 hours of annual free cooling. The
5,600-hour Portland figure means the chilled water plant's compressors are off and only fluid cooler fans are running for
approximately 64% of annual hours — the dominant operating mode, not an occasional benefit.
(cid:127) EC motor optimisation at 60% speed: TMI's EC motor supplier provides efficiency curves for multiple operating speed points. For
Portland, the motor was selected to peak at 60% speed (approximately 35% of rated power) rather than the standard 80% speed
selection. This shifts the motor's highest efficiency region to the actual operating point, reducing motor copper losses by
approximately 8% relative to a standard selection at the same nameplate power.
(cid:127) MERV-13 and wildfire smoke: the ASHRAE Filtration and Air Cleaning Technical Committee recommends MERV-13 minimum for
data centres in the western US fire corridor. Wildfire smoke PM2.5 particles (0.1 to 1.0 micron diameter) penetrate MERV-8 and
MERV-11 media at high rates but are captured at 85 to 90% efficiency by MERV-13. IT equipment exposed to PM2.5 smoke can
experience accelerated metallic corrosion at solder joints and connector pins.
(cid:127) Lead time analysis: TMI's 16-week standard lead time is dominated by the 5-week custom coil fabrication lead. Pre-purchasing the
coils before drawing approval would have reduced total lead time to 11 weeks but exposed TMI to a coil replacement cost of
approximately CAD 30,000 if a post-approval drawing change modified the coil specification. For a project yielding approximately
CAD 24,000 in gross margin, this risk was not commercially viable.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was selecting an EC motor with peak efficiency calibrated at
60% of rated speed rather than the standard 80% selection — reflecting Portland's dominant part-load operating profile — a
specification that reduces annual fan energy consumption by approximately 9,600 kWh per unit at Portland's average operating point
versus a standard selection, prioritising operational cost over the simpler approach of oversizing the motor for peak conditions.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost on lead time. The customer required delivery in 11 weeks to maintain a data centre go-live milestone that triggered a tenant
lease commencement date worth approximately USD 1.2 million in revenue. TMI's standard lead time for this configuration is 16
weeks; after reviewing the production schedule in detail, TMI determined that 13 weeks was achievable with a priority procurement
surcharge but 11 weeks was not achievable without pre-purchasing components before engineering sign-off — a risk TMI was not
willing to accept. The competing manufacturer had a North American fabrication facility with a shorter component procurement lead
time and could commit to 11 weeks.

1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 700 kW sensible — CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C, entering air 23°C DB / 45% RH. San Jose's mild Pacific climate
produces a relatively low design sensible heat ratio; the 45% RH entering condition places modest demands on latent removal. Three
(3) large chilled water AHUs at 350 kW sensible each, N+1: two active, one standby on 168-hour auto-rotation.
Configuration: Floor-mounted chilled water AHUs, hot-aisle containment, cold-aisle underfloor supply. San Jose's mild outdoor
climate makes this an economiser-eligible application: the customer's chilled water plant is designed with a waterside economiser
that provides free cooling when outdoor wet-bulb is below 10°C — approximately 3,800 hours per year in San Jose. TMI's unit
controls are pre-configured to accept an economiser enable signal from the CHW plant.
Cooling coil: 8-row, 12 fins per inch, aluminium-fin copper-tube; face area 1,800 mm x 1,200 mm; CHW supply 7°C / return 14°C.
Standard delta-T coil; San Jose's 45% RH entering condition means latent load is minimal and a standard 11°C leaving dew-point is
more than adequate.
Fan section: two (2) backward-curved EC plenum fans per unit, 38,000 CFM at 0.75 in. w.g., variable-speed drives. San Jose's cool
ambient means the system will operate at low part-load for the majority of the year; EC motor efficiency at 40 to 60% speed is the
primary energy cost driver for this installation.
DCIM integration: N+1 group controller, BACnet IP, 168-hour rotation; supply air temperature and dew-point; RESTful JSON API
over LAN connection. The API exposes supply air temperature, return air temperature, fan speed, valve position, and energy meter
data at 30-second polling intervals. Pre-validated against Nlyte and Sunbird DCIM platforms; compatibility with the customer's
specific platform confirmed at quotation stage.
California Title 24 compliance: fan motor VSD efficiency meets CEC Appliance Efficiency Regulations for variable-speed motor
drives. A Title 24 compliance data sheet is included in the submittal package for building permit purposes.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 100 mm double-wall insulated.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-13. California Title 24 and Santa Clara County air quality regulations require MERV-13 minimum for
mechanical systems in commercial occupancies.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) San Jose ASHRAE 0.4% cooling design: 35°C DB / 21°C WB. The mild summer climate means the cooling system is never
thermally stressed at design conditions. The dominant design consideration is part-load efficiency, because San Jose outdoor DB is
below 25°C for more than 6,000 hours per year.
(cid:127) Waterside economiser availability: San Jose outdoor WB falls below 10°C for approximately 3,800 hours annually. At 10°C WB a
properly designed cooling tower or fluid cooler can deliver CHW supply at 7°C without mechanical compression, saving all
compressor energy during those hours. TMI's unit economiser interface is a standard feature in the California market and adds no
cost.
(cid:127) CEC Title 24 VSD compliance: California's Appliance Efficiency Regulations require fan motor drives for commercial cooling
equipment above 0.75 kW to meet minimum VSD efficiency standards. TMI's EC motor drives meet the current CEC requirements; a
compliance data sheet is included in the submittal package for building permit purposes.
(cid:127) DCIM RESTful API: the group controller exposes a JSON REST API over the LAN connection. The API schema was developed in
coordination with the Nlyte platform and tested against Sunbird; compatibility with other platforms requires customer-side mapping.
The API is a standard feature on all TMI large-format AHUs ordered with the optional DCIM integration package.
(cid:127) Scope escalation trigger: this quotation was originally for 2 active plus 1 standby units at 350 kW each. A design revision added a
fourth unit (3 active plus 1 standby), increasing the total project value above the hyperscaler's preferred-vendor agreement trigger
and changing the procurement pathway from local discretion to mandatory corporate sourcing.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was pre-configuring the group controller with a DCIM RESTful
JSON API and a waterside economiser binary input at the factory — treating DCIM integration and free-cooling readiness as
base-specification features rather than options — to minimise field integration work in a hyperscale environment where the
commissioning window is typically measured in days rather than weeks.
OUTCOME: LOST
Lost due to a hyperscaler preferred-vendor agreement. The customer's parent company had a global master supply agreement with
a competing manufacturer covering all cooling equipment purchases above USD 400,000. The local procurement team had
discretion for projects under this threshold and had intended to award to TMI; a late scope change added one unit, bringing the total
above the USD 400,000 trigger and mandating escalation to corporate procurement, who awarded under the master agreement
without a competitive bid process.

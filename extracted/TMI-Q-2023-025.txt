1. EQUIPMENT SPECIFICATION
Cooling capacity: 3,000 kW sensible — complete Tier 4 facility, Hall A. Fourteen (14) large chilled water AHUs in 2N configuration:
seven active units, seven standby, each capable of independent full-load operation.
Tier 4 dual-CHW-path with arctic provisions: each unit is connected to two fully independent chilled water plants (Plant A and Plant
B). Two independent CHW circuits share the coil matrix. Automatic changeover valve switches between A and B CHW sources within
5 seconds. Arctic heat trace on all CHW connection stubs exposed to the building exterior — CHW connections pass through an
exterior mechanical room wall and require heat trace to prevent freezing at Toronto's winter design of -23°C DB.
Cooling coil: 10-row, 14 fins per inch, aluminium-fin copper-tube; two independent CHW circuits; face area 2,200 mm x 1,500 mm;
CHW supply 6°C / return 13°C. The 6°C supply achieves a 10°C leaving dew-point — appropriate for Toronto's summer humidity
peaks when outdoor WB can reach 24°C.
Waterside economiser: modulating bypass valve per unit, active when outdoor WB is below 8°C — approximately 4,200 hours per
year in Toronto. At 4,200 annual hours this is the highest economiser utilisation of any North American project in TMI's reference
portfolio; compressor energy savings are substantial at campus scale.
Fan section: four (4) EC backward-curved fans per unit, 50,000 CFM total at 0.90 in. w.g., dual-power-feed motor connections for 2N
electrical compliance.
Controls: 2N dual-processor group controller, BACnet IP, redundant network; CHW A/B changeover within 5 seconds; arctic
cold-start logic; economiser management. Bilingual English/French alarm messages throughout.
Cabinet: 14-gauge galvanised steel, 100 mm double-wall arctic insulated, SDC B seismic. CSA C22.2 electrical certification and
bilingual O&M; documentation included as standard for Canadian market.
Filtration: 4-inch MERV-13, redundant dual-track for online filter replacement without airflow interruption.

7. ENGINEERING NOTES & ASSUMPTIONS
(cid:127) Toronto winter design: -23°C DB (ASHRAE 99.6%). Arctic heat trace on CHW connection stubs is the critical cold-weather
protection feature for a 2N chilled water system in Toronto. Both the A-circuit and B-circuit stubs require independent heat trace
circuits — loss of heat trace on either stub during a winter building pressurisation event can freeze that circuit independently of the
other.
(cid:127) Waterside economiser at 4,200 annual hours: Toronto outdoor WB falls below 8°C for approximately 4,200 hours annually — more
than any North American TMI installation in the reference portfolio. During these hours the chilled water plant compressors are fully
bypassed and only fluid cooler fan energy is consumed. Annual compressor energy saving per unit is approximately 240 MWh,
yielding a waterside economiser payback of less than 2 years at Canadian grid electricity rates.
(cid:127) 2N changeover valve achieves A-to-B switchover within 5 seconds: during switchover, no cooling interruption occurs because both
circuits are pre-charged and the coil thermal mass maintains supply air temperature within 2°C of setpoint throughout the transition.
This was verified at factory test by interrupting Plant A CHW flow on a fully loaded unit and measuring supply air temperature
deviation.
(cid:127) Bilingual alarm messages and CSA C22.2 certification are standard on all TMI units for the Canadian market and add no cost. CSA
certification is required by the Ontario Building Code for mechanical equipment with factory-assembled electrical panels; the
certification documentation is included in the building permit package.
(cid:127) SR&ED; eligibility: the dual-circuit arctic coil design incorporates novel thermal engineering that may qualify for Canadian federal
Scientific Research and Experimental Development (SR&ED;) tax credits; TMI can provide a project cost breakdown in
SR&ED-compatible; format at the customer's request.
Critical design decision: The critical design decision on this project was specifying independent arctic heat trace circuits on both
the A-side and B-side CHW connection stubs rather than a single shared heat trace loop, because a failure of a single shared heat
trace loop during a Toronto -23°C winter event could freeze both CHW circuits simultaneously — defeating the 2N redundancy and
creating the exact dual-failure mode the Tier 4 architecture is designed to prevent.
